Designing the Ramp (Spiral Design Cycle)

For this project, I will be following the spiral design cycle as described in this document

Mr. Rice had aready made a prototype ramp. It consists of two side walls with a 'living joint' (a flat sheet of MDF with many slots cut in it to make it flexible) to form the curve of the ramp. These are held together with finger joints (a.k.a. tabs) For my project I made a new and improved version, using the same idea but tweaking and adding to it.

The origional ramp had large tabs holding it to the side wall every few inches, which left some spots unsupported. I generated the idea of having smaller tas at every point of contact.

The next step was to design this change in CAD

Building this ramp was the really fun part. I learned to use the CNC router to cut the parts, then spent half an hour wrangling them so that I could add wood screws to hold the ramp together.

(it turns out that a floppy sheet of wood is rather unwieldly)

Once the ramp was together, it was time to evaluate it. It was already very solid (an improvement over the first model), but there were a couple changes to be made before calling it a finished product.

First, the end of the ramp extended below the base of the side wall, so it could not sit flat

Second, the design of the ramp meant that the top surface had thin gaps were material had been cut away, producing an uneven surface

Generating ideas to fix these issues was fairly strghtforward. making the ramp sit flat was simply a matter of removing some material. fpr smoothing it out, a thin sheet of smooth plastic (celluplar PVC) over the top of the ramp was a quick fix.

This step took very little design. All that it needed was a rectangular piece of plastic sized to match the width and length of the ramp

To build the next 'iteration', I first took a kacksaw and chisel to my nice new ramp to remove the offending material from the bottom. Then, (with assistance) I cut a piece of cellular PVC on the bandsaw to fit over the top surface of the ramp, then put it in place and added a screw to hold it there.

At that point, I took a single, blurry picture to document the frinal product :(

When evaluating the ramp for the second time, I finally got to roll the car down it a few times. It worked quite well; the car rolled cleanly down the ramp and made a smooth transition onto the floor.
